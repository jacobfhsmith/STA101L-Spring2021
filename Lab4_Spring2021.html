Sampling distributions

A sample statistic is simply a function of a random sample. Some examples include the sample mean (sum the sample values and divide by the sample size) and sample median (order the samples and find the middle value). The distribution of a statistic is called a sampling distribution. For example, compare the two statements:

  1. â€œWhatâ€™s the chance that the lifetime of a lightbulb is greater than 750 hours?â€

  2. â€œWhatâ€™s the chance that the average lifetime of 100 lightbulbs is greater than 750 hours?â€

These two statements are similar (â€˜the chance some number is greater than 750â€™), but the top concerns a single observation while the bottom concerns 100 samples. To answer the bottom question, we will need to know the distribution of the sample mean.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Question one can often be very challenging to directly answer while question two can be very easy (provided we sample enough lightbulbs). This is due to the Central Limit Theorem (CLT). The CLT basically states that the distribution of the sample mean will be approximately normal for a large enough sample size. In the context above, this means that the distribution of the sample average of 100 (or 100,000) lightbulbs will look normally distributed, even though the distribution of a single lightbulb might be highly skewed and non-normal.

Standard error

The standard error is simply the standard deviation of the sampling distribution.

For example, if we kept taking 100 lightbulbs off of a factory line and computing the average lifetime, we would expect the average lifetime itself to vary between samples of size 100. This variability is due to the randomness of the individual lightbulbs, so the variability of the sample mean and the individual lightbulbs is related, but not exactly the same. To avoid confusion, we will call the standard deviation of the average lifetime the standard error and the standard deviation of an individual lightbulbâ€¦wellâ€¦the standard deviation.

Looking at the density plot above, do you think DemVotes is normally distributed? Take a look at the two histograms below. What happens as the sample size \(n\) increases? Admittedly, the blue histogram corresponding to \(n = 100\) is still skewed to the right. Also, DemVotes can only take positive values, while a random variable that is normally distributed can take both positive and negative values. # Remember to type ?rep_sample_n in the console to see what each argument of the function does!
sample_size_10 = rep_sample_n(tbl = election_Georgia,size = 10, replace = T, reps = 5000) %>% 
                 summarise(sample_mean = mean(DemVotes), .groups='drop') 

sample_size_100 = rep_sample_n(tbl = election_Georgia,size = 100, replace = T, reps = 5000) %>% 
                 summarise(sample_mean = mean(DemVotes), .groups='drop') 

ggplot() + geom_histogram(data=sample_size_10,aes(x=sample_mean),fill='red',alpha=0.2,bins=50) + 
           geom_histogram(data=sample_size_100,aes(x=sample_mean),fill='blue',alpha=0.2,bins=50)

Normal Distribution function in R Example

In exercise 1, you will become familiar with various â€œnormal distribution functionsâ€ in R. These include

  • pnorm(): Calculate the probability a normal random variable falls within a specified region.
  • rnorm(): Generate random numbers from a normal distribution.
  • qnorm(): Return the value under a normal distribution corresponding to a given probability (the â€˜inverseâ€™ of pnorm()).

Refer to https://www.statology.org/dnorm-pnorm-rnorm-qnorm-in-r/ for more information.

Hereâ€™s an example: Suppose \(X\) is a random variable that is normally distributed with mean \(\mu = 10\) and standard deviation \(\sigma = 1\) (people sometimes just write this statement as \(X \sim N(10,1)\)). The distribution of \(X\) looks like

  1. The pnorm() function allows us to calculate probabilities. For example, what is \(P(X > 12)\), the probability that \(X\) is greater than 12? This probability is the area of the red region, which is in the â€˜upper tailâ€™ of the distribution. Because of this, we set â€˜lower.tail = FALSEâ€™ in the pnorm() function (see below). To find the area of the green region (denoted by \(P(X \leq 9)\)), we set â€˜lower.tail=TRUEâ€™. Notice that the blue region is the complement of the red and green regions.
# Area of red region:
pnorm(q=12,mean=10,sd=1,lower.tail = FALSE)
# Area of green region:
pnorm(q=9,mean=10,sd=1,lower.tail = TRUE)
# "Area of blue region = 1 - Area of red region - Area of green region: "
1 - pnorm(q=12,mean=10,sd=1,lower.tail = FALSE) - pnorm(q=9,mean=10,sd=1,lower.tail = TRUE)
  1. The qnorm() function gives us the value that corresponds to a given probability. What value of \(X\) corresponds to a lower tail area of \(0.1586553\)? If you ran the code above, then you know that the answer is 9. This is also called the \(15.86553^{\text{th}}\) percentile under this distribution. Make sure you specify the tail direction!
qnorm(p=0.1586553,mean=10,sd=1,lower.tail = TRUE)
  1. To simulate values of \(X\), use rnorm(). The \(n\) argument specifies how many simulations you want.
Simulations = rnorm(n=500,mean=10,sd=1)
